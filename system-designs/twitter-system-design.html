What we're building & what's different from Instagram

We'll design a simplified Twitter. If you've been through the Instagram guide, great — the skeleton (load balancers, sharded databases, caches, hybrid feeds) carries over, and we'll recap it quickly rather than re-derive it. The genuinely new material here is: tweets are tiny text (changes the storage math), the celebrity problem is far worse, and — our special focus — a full advertising system: how promoted tweets get targeted, auctioned, injected into your timeline, and billed.

Meet our cast

  • Maya — regular user, 400 followers, tweets hot takes about cricket.
  • Rohan — mostly scrolls his timeline; rarely tweets.
  • Zayn — celebrity, 60 million followers.
  • Stride Shoes — a sneaker brand that wants to advertise to sports fans like Maya and Rohan. (New character type: the advertiser!)

💡 Key insights to state early
  • Even more read-heavy than Instagram. Twitter's famous stat: ~1000x more timeline reads than tweet writes. Optimize reads at all costs.
  • Tweets are tiny. ~300 bytes of text vs. Instagram's 200 KB photos. Text storage is almost free — which means we can afford to copy tweets around aggressively (this unlocks the timeline design).
  • Ads have different consistency needs. A late tweet is fine; charging Stride Shoes for clicks that didn't happen is not. One system, two consistency standards — a great interview talking point.

Back-of-the-envelope math

Assume 200M daily active users, 400M tweets/day:

  • Tweet writes: 400M/day ≈ 4,600 tweets/sec (peaks 3–4x higher)
  • Text storage: 400M × 300 bytes ≈ 120 GB/day — trivially small! (Media is separate, in object storage.)
  • Timeline reads: ~1000:1 ratio → millions of reads/sec at peak → caching is not optional, it IS the design.

Step 2

The home timeline — Twitter's signature problem

Historical note worth dropping in your interview: the pull/push/hybrid timeline pattern is Twitter's contribution to system design folklore. In its early "fail whale" years, Twitter computed timelines on-the-fly (pull) and kept falling over. The fix — precomputed timelines — is now the textbook answer.

⏪ Recap: the three approaches
  • Pull (fan-out on read): when Rohan opens the app, query recent tweets from all 500 people he follows, merge, sort. Slow — blows the 200ms budget, repeats work every refresh.
  • Push (fan-out on write): keep a precomputed timeline list per user in a RAM cache. When Maya tweets, workers insert her tweet ID into all 400 followers' lists (via a message queue, asynchronously). Reads become one ~1ms cache lookup.
  • Celebrity problem: Zayn tweets → 60 million list inserts. Too much. So: hybrid — push for normal users, pull-and-merge at read time for the few celebrities you follow.

Why Twitter can be even more aggressive with push than Instagram: a timeline entry is just a tiny tweet ID (8 bytes), and tweets themselves are ~300 bytes. Copying a tweet reference into 400 followers' lists costs almost nothing. Cheap data → copy freely → instant reads. (With 200 KB photos you'd only copy IDs too — but Twitter can even cache the full tweet text in RAM for the hottest tweets.)

🎬 Real-world walkthrough

Maya tweets "What a catch!!" during a cricket final. Within ~2 seconds, fan-out workers have slipped tweet t812 into 400 followers' cached timelines. Rohan refreshes: his precomputed list comes back in 1ms, the service pulls Zayn's latest tweet (celebrity → not fanned out), merges by timestamp, fetches tweet text (mostly cache hits), and responds — 40ms total. Meanwhile a user whose timeline cache node just failed gets rebuilt from the database — a rare slow request, not an outage.

✓ Takeaway

Hybrid fan-out + timelines living in RAM. But notice: everything so far shows tweets from people you chose to follow. Ads break that assumption — someone pays to enter your timeline. That needs entirely new machinery.

Step 3 — new territory

The Ads system, part 1: advertisers, targeting & the auction

Twitter is free for Maya and Rohan because Stride Shoes pays. Let's build the advertiser side first: how an ad campaign is created and how we decide which ad wins a slot in Rohan's timeline.

What an advertiser creates: a campaign

Campaign                          Ad (promoted tweet)
──────────────────────            ─────────────────────
campaign_id (key)                 ad_id (key)
advertiser_id (Stride Shoes)      campaign_id
total_budget   ($10,000)          creative = an ordinary tweet!
bid            ($0.50 per click)  ("Meet the new Stride Air 👟")
targeting      (interests=sports,
                location=India,
                age=18-35)
start/end dates

Note the elegant trick: a promoted tweet is just a normal tweet with a campaign attached. It reuses our whole tweet infrastructure — storage, media, rendering. New tables, not a new universe.

📚 Concept: Targeting & user profiles

Advertisers don't pick users; they pick audiences ("sports fans in India, 18–35"). So we maintain a user-profile store: for each user, a set of features — location, age band, inferred interests. Where do interests come from? Behavior: Rohan follows 40 cricket accounts and clicks cricket tweets, so a background job tags him interests: [cricket, sneakers]. This store is fed by the same event stream we'll build in Step 4, and it's read at ad-serving time to match users to campaigns.

📚 Concept: The ad auction

Usually many campaigns target Rohan at once. Who gets the slot? An auction, run in milliseconds at request time:

  1. Candidate selection: find all active campaigns whose targeting matches Rohan's profile and that still have budget left (say, 200 candidates).
  2. Scoring: for each, compute expected value = bid × pCTR, where pCTR ("predicted click-through rate") is a machine-learning model's guess at how likely Rohan specifically is to click this specific ad. Why multiply? We're paid per click — an ad with a $2 bid nobody clicks earns less than a $0.50 bid everyone clicks. Auctioning on bid × pCTR maximizes revenue AND relevance at once.
  3. Winner & price: highest score wins. Commonly a second-price auction: the winner pays just enough to beat #2, not their full bid — which encourages honest bidding (you can bid your true value without fear of overpaying).

Analogy: a billboard beside the highway, re-auctioned for every single passing car, to the advertiser whose message that particular driver is most likely to act on. That's the absurd, wonderful scale of online ads.

🎬 Real-world walkthrough

Stride Shoes sets up: $10,000 budget, $0.50/click, targeting India · 18–35 · interests: sports. That night, Rohan (tagged cricket, sneakers) opens his timeline. The auction considers 200 matching campaigns. Stride bids $0.50 with pCTR 8% → score 0.04. A cola brand bids $2.00 but its pCTR for Rohan is only 1% → score 0.02. Stride wins despite the 4x smaller bid — because Rohan actually cares about sneakers. He's charged-for only if he clicks.

✓ Takeaway

Ads side adds: campaign/ad tables, a user-profile store for targeting, and a real-time auction ranking by bid × pCTR. Next: wiring this into the timeline within our 200ms budget, and the money pipeline.

  • The timeline service calls the ad server in parallel with assembling organic tweets, giving it a strict ~50ms slice of our 200ms budget.
  • Graceful degradation: if the ad server is slow or down, the timeline ships without ads. We lose a few cents of revenue; Rohan never sees an error. (Interviewers love this point: decide per-component what failure should look like.)
  • Winning ads are interleaved at fixed gaps — enough to earn revenue, not so many that users quit.

Tracking: impressions & clicks

📚 Concept: Event pipeline (stream processing)

Every time an ad is shown, the app fires an impression event; every tap fires a click event. At millions/sec, you never write these straight to a database. Instead they flow into a message queue built for firehoses (Kafka), and stream processors (Flink/Spark) consume them to continuously update: per-campaign spend, impression/click counts, and the training data for the pCTR model. It's the same "queue + workers" pattern from fan-out, scaled up to be the backbone of all analytics.

🎬 Real-world walkthrough — following one click's money

Rohan taps the Stride ad. His app fires click(ad_id, user, time) → Kafka. Within seconds a stream processor: (1) runs fraud checks — is this a bot? the same user clicking 50 times? (discarded if so); (2) adds $0.43 (the second-price amount) to Stride's spend counter; (3) updates the campaign dashboard Stride's marketing team is watching. When spend hits $10,000, the campaign is flagged exhausted and drops out of future auctions within seconds.

⚠ Two ads-specific hard problems (great interview material)
  • Budget overspend: thousands of ad servers auction simultaneously; if each checks a stale budget, Stride could be charged $10,300 on a $10,000 budget. Fix: near-real-time spend counters + campaigns are throttled as they approach their cap; small overshoots are typically not billed (eaten by the platform).
  • Two consistency standards in one system: timelines are eventually consistent (a late tweet is fine), but billing must be exact — every click counted once, exactly once, auditable. So billing events land in durable storage with idempotency keys (a retry of the same click event doesn't double-charge) and are reconciled by batch jobs. Same architecture, stricter guarantees where money flows.
✓ Takeaway

Serving: separate ad server, parallel call, strict time budget, degrade to ad-free rather than fail. Money: click → Kafka → fraud filter → spend counter → exact, idempotent billing. Eventual consistency for content, strict consistency for money.

Step 5 — quick bonus

Search & Trending (brief, but interviewers often probe here)

Search: the inverted index

📚 Concept: Inverted index

To answer "show me tweets containing cricket," scanning 400M tweets/day is hopeless. An inverted index flips the data: instead of tweet → words, store word → list of tweet IDs ("cricket" → [t812, t790, …]). Exactly like the index at the back of a textbook. When Maya tweets, a worker (fed by the same message queue as fan-out) tokenizes her tweet and appends its ID to each word's list. Search = look up the word, fetch the tweets. Elasticsearch/Lucene is the standard tool.

Trending topics: counting in windows

📚 Concept: Sliding-window counting

"Trending" = hashtags with a sudden spike recently. The tweet event stream (Kafka again!) feeds a stream processor that counts hashtag mentions in, say, 5-minute windows, and compares against each tag's normal baseline. #CricketFinal jumping from 100 to 90,000 mentions/window → trending. Notice the pattern: one event stream, many consumers — fan-out workers, the search indexer, trend counters, ad-spend counters, and the pCTR trainer all drink from the same firehose. That's why the queue is the true center of the architecture.

✓ Takeaway

Search = inverted index, updated asynchronously. Trending = stream-processed sliding-window counts. Both are just more consumers of the tweet event stream.

Flow 1 — Maya tweets (write path)

  1. App → LB → Tweet Service: store text in Tweet DB shard (Snowflake ID), media to object storage, publish "Maya tweeted t812" to Kafka. Ack Maya immediately.
  2. Consumers do the rest async: fan-out to 400 timelines, index words for search, bump hashtag counters.

Flow 2 — Rohan opens his timeline (read path, <200ms, with ads)

  1. Timeline Service reads Rohan's precomputed list from cache (~1ms), pulls + merges celebrity tweets (Zayn).
  2. In parallel, Ad Server matches Rohan's profile → auction → returns 2 winning promoted tweets (≤50ms, or timeline ships ad-free).
  3. Ads interleaved every ~7 posts; tweet texts fetched (mostly cache hits); media loads via CDN.
  4. The app fires impression events for the ads shown → Kafka → spend/pCTR pipelines.

New glossary entries (beyond the Instagram guide)
  • Impression / click: an ad being shown / being tapped — the two billable-ish events.
  • CTR / pCTR: click-through rate; the ML-predicted probability a given user clicks a given ad.
  • Ad auction: per-request contest ranked by bid × pCTR; second-price = winner pays just enough to beat #2.
  • Budget pacing: throttling a campaign so it spends evenly and never blows past its cap.
  • Idempotency: processing the same event twice has no extra effect — the key to never double-charging.
  • Graceful degradation: when a subsystem fails, ship a reduced experience (ad-free timeline) instead of an error.
  • Inverted index: word → list of documents; how search avoids scanning everything.
  • Stream processing: continuously computing over an event firehose (Kafka + Flink) — powers fan-out, trends, spend, ML training.
  • Snowflake ID: timestamp+machine+counter ID scheme (born at Twitter); sortable by time, no central counter.
